About Me

The Person Behind
the Pixels

Design has been part of my life for as long as I can remember. Growing up, I looked up to my uncle — a clay modeller at Hero — and spent hours filling notebooks with car sketches, trying to capture every curve and line.

In college, while everyone around me was learning web development, I found myself obsessed with making games. There was something about building interactive worlds that felt more real to me than anything else.

"After working in the game industry, I realised I could do more — not just build what designers imagine, but become the one imagining."

That's why I'm here. I want to learn design properly, gain real experience, and eventually open my own game design studio. That's the long-term dream — a place where great design and great engineering live under the same roof.

Featured Case Study

XR Indian Road Simulator

A high-fidelity VR simulator built to train cognitive reflexes against real-world chaos.

Role: XR Developer & UX Researcher Timeline: 1 Week (Sprint) Tech: Unity XR, C#, Blosm OSM

1. The Core Problem: The Illusion of Licensing

In India, the driving licensing infrastructure is fundamentally disconnected from reality. Millions of riders are legally cleared to ride motorcycles simply by successfully navigating a primary "Figure-8" track on pristine, artificial roads.

The real-world scenario is vastly different. Real Indian roads are saturated with chaotic variables: deep potholes, unauthorized blind speedbreakers, stray animals, sudden barricades, and highly unpredictable, unlicensed traffic. Because riders aren't trained for these multi-modal stress factors, accident rates skyrocket.

2. The Solution & Ideation

To mitigate these accidents, we need safe, accessible scenario exposure. The objective of this project was to establish a high-fidelity VR simulator that allows Indian riders to train their cognitive reflexes against real-world chaos from the safety of their homes.

Instead of relying on gamepads—which build zero real-world muscle memory—I mapped out a completely intuitive XR control scheme. In my ideation phase, I conceptualized turning the standard VR controllers into 1:1 physical handlebars, tracking hand position to actively balance the bike, twist for acceleration, and pull for braking.

Initial paper ideation resolving the cognitive mapping of standard VR controllers to physical scooter handlebars.

3. Prototyping Embodied Interactions

Prototyping this in Unity required abandoning standard "arcade" driving mechanics in favor of high-fidelity, embodied vehicle physics.

  • VR Handlebar Dynamics: By locking the user's virtual hands to the bike's handlebars, the XR controllers act as dual throttles and brakes. The spatial translation of pulling the controllers left or right handles the leaning and balancing of the chassis, offering a 1:1 cognitive mapping to riding an actual bike.
  • Accurate Drivetrain: I engineered a realistic multi-gear system with engine-lugging mechanics. If a rider fails to downshift at low speeds—a common mistake for beginners that leads to stalling in intersections—the engine physically shudders, shaking the VR camera to train spatial awareness of the vehicle's state.

4. Art Direction for Cognitive Load

When importing accurate 1:1 map data of Jodhpur, Ajmer, and Gandhinagar into VR, I made a critical UX decision to avoid photorealism. In a highly chaotic VR environment, photorealistic texturing overwhelms the visual cortex, leading to motion sickness.

I developed a "Sable-inspired" minimalist, flat-shaded art direction. By utilizing flat architectural coloring (such as iconic Jodhpur Blue) masked with thin ink borders, I radically reduced background visual noise. This empowers the rider's brain to instantly distinguish between safe street geometry and incoming active hazards.

Stylized Application

5. UI Evolution: Removing the Arcades

Early iterations of the simulator featured heavy, blocky diegetic overlays (like giant speed dials hovering in space). Iterative testing revealed that opaque UI blocked critical spatial threats in the rider's peripheral vision. I overhauled the Canvas structure, stripping away all block backgrounds in favor of an architectural typographic hierarchy that sits flush against the corners of the screen—ensuring nothing stands between the rider and the road.

The new transparent, typographic minimal-HUD.

Reflection

The final prototype successfully bridges the gap between static licensing tests and the unpredictable reality of Indian driving. It trains muscle memory, enforces cognitive hazard response, and delivers a comfortable, deeply stylized VR experience designed to save lives on the road.

Paintings & Drawings

A timeline of learning to see

April 2019

Ducati Panigale V4

Pencil on Paper

This is where it all started. I'd been sketching cars and bikes since I was a kid, trying to capture every mechanical detail. This Ducati was one of those drawings where I sat down and didn't get up until it was done. It's not paint on canvas — just graphite and obsession.

May 2019

Leopard

Acrylic on Canvas

My personal favourite. This was one of my first real paintings — moving from pencil to paint felt like learning a new language. The spots, the textures, the way light catches fur — it taught me that details aren't decoration. They're what make something feel alive.

October 2020

Moonlit Solitude

Acrylic on Canvas

This one came out of lockdown — long nights, a lot of silence. I wanted to paint something that felt quiet and heavy at the same time. The Bandhani dupatta was the hardest part, getting those white circles to feel hand-printed rather than stamped.

April 2022

Hanuman — Sanjivani

Acrylic on Canvas · Inspired by Raja Ravi Verma

This was the painting where I discovered Raja Ravi Verma's work. The way he brought mythology to life — with real weight and emotion — completely changed how I thought about painting. I wanted Hanuman to feel powerful but grounded, divine but human. It's the most ambitious thing I've painted.

August 2025

Soumya

Acrylic on Canvas

My latest — a portrait of my sister. Portraits are the hardest because you can't hide behind style. If the eyes are off by a millimeter, everyone knows. This one took the longest, but it's also the one where I finally felt like I could see the person, not just the shapes.
