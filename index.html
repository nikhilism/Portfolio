About Me

The Person Behind
the Pixels

Design has been part of my life for as long as I can remember. Growing up, I looked up to my uncle — a clay modeller at Hero — and spent hours filling notebooks with car sketches, trying to capture every curve and line.

In college, while everyone around me was learning web development, I found myself obsessed with making games. There was something about building interactive worlds that felt more real to me than anything else.

"After working in the game industry, I realised I could do more — not just build what designers imagine, but become the one imagining."

That's why I'm here. I want to learn design properly, gain real experience, and eventually open my own game design studio. That's the long-term dream — a place where great design and great engineering live under the same roof.

Featured Case Study

XR Indian Road Simulator

A VR simulator that puts you in the middle of real Indian road chaos — before you actually have to face it.

Role: XR Developer & UX Researcher Timeline: 1 Week (Sprint) Tech: Unity XR, C#, Blosm OSM

1. The Core Problem

Getting a license in India often feels completely disconnected from what it's actually like to ride here. Millions of riders pass oversimplified maneuverability tests on artificial, closed-course tracks, and are practically immediately cleared for the real world.

But the real world is chaotic. We deal with deep potholes, blind sudden speedbreakers, wandering animals, and completely unpredictable traffic. Traditional tests don't prep riders for any of this, which is a huge factor in why accident rates skyrocket on Indian roads.

2. The Idea: A Safe Space to Fail

The answer felt obvious once I framed it right — put riders inside the chaos before sending them into it. A VR simulator where you can dump the bike, miss the speedbreaker, freeze at the intersection, and start over. No injuries. Just learning.

The first thing I knew for sure: gamepads wouldn't work. A thumbstick doesn't teach your wrists anything. So I redesigned the input entirely — standard VR controllers become handlebars. You lean to steer, twist to accelerate, pull to brake. Then I took it one step further: I tied the two controllers together with a physical rope. When you hold them properly, the rope goes taut — just like gripping real handlebars. Go slack, and the controls stop responding. It's a small thing, but it completely changes how your body holds itself.

Early sketches figuring out how to turn standard VR controllers into something that actually feels like a scooter.

3. Nailing the Physics (and the Feel)

Before any of the city building, before any visuals, I just needed the bike to feel right. I made a dead-simple test map — just a flat plane with a few turns — and spent most of that time riding around in circles, tuning until it stopped feeling like an arcade game.

The scrappy test map where I spent most of the first two days just getting the lean to feel honest.

  • Handlebar Leaning: Locking your virtual hands to the bike and using hand position to steer — it sounds simple, but the moment it clicked, it genuinely felt like riding a real bike.
  • Engine Lugging: Forget to downshift as you slow down — the bike shudders and the camera shakes. Annoying in the sim. Exactly what happens in real life, which is the whole point.

4. Making the World Actually Feel Like Something

I started by pulling real Jodhpur street data into Blender and importing it into Unity. Which sounds great — until you see it. Raw map data is just grey boxes and flat roads. No sense of place, no character, nothing. It looked more like a loading screen than a city.

Processing the real-world Jodhpur street data inside Blender.

The initial engine view: dull geometry before applying any art direction.

I tried realistic textures first. It looked bad and made me motion sick within minutes — which, in VR, is a design failure, not a hardware problem. Too much visual noise makes your brain work overtime just to parse the scene, before a single hazard even shows up.

So I stripped it back. Flat shading, bold outlines, and Jodhpur's own Blue as the base colour for the buildings. The city starts reading like itself immediately — and more importantly, anything moving stands out. Your eye goes straight to the cow that just stepped off the footpath.

This build only covers the Jodhpur map for now. Next comes the real chaos: live traffic, stray animals, sudden pedestrians, potholes that appear right when you least expect them.

5. The UI — Staying Out of the Way

Once the world felt right, I had one last thing to figure out: where does the information go? Speed, gear, score — the rider needs all of it, but none of it should eat up their field of view on a busy junction.

I pushed the stats to the far corners and kept the dashboard dials right on the bike where you'd naturally glance anyway. The road stays clear. Your eye doesn't have to split between a HUD and the auto that just cut you off.

The final build — flat-shaded Jodhpur streets, real map geometry, and a UI that gets out of the rider's way.

Reflection

What started as a frustration — watching riders get licensed without ever seeing real traffic — turned into something I genuinely believe in. It's not finished. But the core of it works: you come out of a session more aware, more tense in the right places, your hands remembering what it felt like to hold on.

Beyond The Screen

Paintings & Drawings

A timeline of learning to see

April 2019

Ducati Panigale V4

Pencil on Paper

This is where it all started. I'd been sketching cars and bikes since I was a kid, trying to capture every mechanical detail. This Ducati was one of those drawings where I sat down and didn't get up until it was done. It's not paint on canvas — just graphite and obsession.

May 2019

Leopard

Acrylic on Canvas

My personal favourite. This was one of my first real paintings — moving from pencil to paint felt like learning a new language. The spots, the textures, the way light catches fur — it taught me that details aren't decoration. They're what make something feel alive.

October 2020

Moonlit Solitude

Acrylic on Canvas

This one came out of lockdown — long nights, a lot of silence. I wanted to paint something that felt quiet and heavy at the same time. The Bandhani dupatta was the hardest part, getting those white circles to feel hand-printed rather than stamped.

April 2022

Hanuman — Sanjivani

Acrylic on Canvas · Inspired by Raja Ravi Verma

This was the painting where I discovered Raja Ravi Verma's work. The way he brought mythology to life — with real weight and emotion — completely changed how I thought about painting. I wanted Hanuman to feel powerful but grounded, divine but human. It's the most ambitious thing I've painted.

August 2025

Soumya

Acrylic on Canvas

My latest — a portrait of my sister. Portraits are the hardest because you can't hide behind style. If the eyes are off by a millimeter, everyone knows. This one took the longest, but it's also the one where I finally felt like I could see the person, not just the shapes.
